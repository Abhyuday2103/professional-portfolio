Artifact 01

Professional self-assessment

Introduction

Before you can lead anyone else through a change, you have to run an honest audit on yourself. This artifact is that audit, and I am leading my portfolio with it on purpose. A portfolio that only shows wins is a brochure. This one starts with the gaps.

Description

In Workshop One I completed two diagnostic self-assessments, one measuring readiness to integrate AI and machine learning into an organization, the other measuring change management capability. I then analyzed the results against the course readings and wrote a candid account of where I am strong, where I am not, and where the instruments themselves fall short.

Objective

To set a baseline I can return to later, to name my real growth areas instead of the comfortable ones, and to show that I can assess my own capability with the same rigor I bring to a sales territory or a key account.

Process

The two forms revealed different things because I scored so differently on each. On the AI/ML integration assessment my strengths were promoting the value of AI and ML, crafting a vision for adoption, and keeping up with the field, which tracks with years spent selling stone to skeptical fabricators, a job that is essentially evangelism. My growth areas were ethical awareness, preparing people for AI-driven change, and understanding data requirements. The ethics one stung because it is true. I have built tools at work, including a pricing calculator the sales team uses, and I only ever asked whether they worked, never what could go wrong.

On the change management assessment my strengths were stakeholder engagement, performance evaluation, and problem-solving, all sharpened by managing a small team and learning to read where people actually are versus where they say they are. My growth areas were formal frameworks, risk management, and facing resistance head-on. I have led change by instinct but could not have named a single framework before this course, and my instinct when someone resists is to route around them rather than through them. The Center for Creative Leadership (2024) names exactly this, that unsuccessful change leaders avoid difficult stakeholders instead of influencing them, and I recognized myself in the line.

My read on the instruments is mixed. Their value, as Change Strategists (2023) notes, is creating a benchmark you can return to later. But both measure self-perception, not behavior, so I could rate myself a 5 on everything and no one would know the difference. I caught my own inconsistency, rating ethical awareness a 1 while scoring 16 out of 20 on the dedicated ethics section, which tells me the overlapping items are not measuring cleanly.

If I revised them I would require a one-sentence example behind every rating, since Change Strategists (2023) ties real assessment to behavioral evidence and past performance, and a bare number invites wishful thinking. I would also add the most important finding from the Center for Creative Leadership (2024): whether you communicate the why behind a change, not just the what. In my experience connecting a change to what people already care about is the entire game, and neither form asks about it.

Tools and technologies used

AI/ML Integration Readiness self-assessment, the Change Management capability self-assessment, and Claude Fable 5 (Anthropic), which I used to help organize the analysis. The scores, workplace examples, and conclusions are my own, and I reviewed and edited the final content to make sure it represents my views.

Value proposition

Unique value. Most people cannot name their own blind spots. This artifact shows that I can, and that I use frameworks and evidence to do it rather than gut feel. That is the same discipline that turns a self-assessment into a development plan and a sales gap into a closed deal.
Relevance. For any employer or partner betting on me to lead change, whether that means adopting AI tools or expanding a business into a new market, the first question is whether I know what I do not know. This is the proof.

Ranked this way the shape is hard to miss, and it is not the shape I would have predicted. Every category that depends on reading and handling people sits at the top: assessing what someone is actually capable of, building trust, staying self-aware, guiding a team through a transition. Every category that depends on formal process sits at the bottom: risk, resistance, organizational culture, and knowledge of the frameworks themselves. I have been leading change on instinct and relationship, and the numbers say so.

Putting the two sheets next to each other produced the finding I did not expect. On the change management form I never drop below 67 percent. On the AI/ML form my weakest category by a wide margin is change readiness for AI/ML integration, at 8 out of 20, and the single lowest statement on it is preparing employees for AI-related change, which I rated a 1. So I am comparatively good at leading people through change and comparatively bad at leading people through this change. The general skill did not transfer to the specific case, and that gap is the most useful thing either form told me.

Artifact 02

Podium Pal, an AI speaker matching tool

Introduction

Artifact 01 is me looking inward. This one points the other way: a working AI product, built with an eight-person team, live on the web, that a stranger can use right now. I chose it as my second artifact because the AI Lab was the first time in this program I shipped something instead of writing about something.

Description

Podium Pal is a speaker bureau tool built by an eight-person team for the AI Lab. It matches event organizers with TED-style speakers by topic, language, and budget. The build is a single-page application with keyword search, price filters, an AI chat layer, and a booking flow, deployed live on GitHub Pages.

Objective

Replace the brochure. A speaker bureau normally hands you a list of profiles and leaves the matching to you, which is backwards, because matching is the entire service. We wanted a visitor to state what their event needs and get an answer.

Process

My assigned job was the marketing infographic. I ended up building and deploying the bot as well: the keyword search, the price filters, the chat layer, and the booking flow.

The decision I would defend hardest is not a feature, it is the business model. We started with a subscription and killed it once we were honest about how rarely anyone books a speaker. A subscription prices for a frequency that does not exist. We moved to commission per booking with payments flowing through the platform, which is the Airbnb answer to the obvious problem that both sides have every incentive to meet once and then transact around you.

The other lesson was that shipping is a skill of its own. The name changed three times, the pricing copy contradicted itself twice, and the first deploy returned a 404. None of that is glamorous and all of it is the job. Catching those inconsistencies and fixing them fast turned out to be most of the actual work.

I also drew the podium mascot with the waving arm. He tested well.

Tools and technologies used

A single-page web application built with HTML, CSS, and JavaScript, carrying keyword search, budget filtering, a conversational AI chat layer for matching, and a booking flow. Deployed on GitHub Pages. Claude (Anthropic) supported development. The product decisions and business model are the team's own.

Value proposition

Unique value. I was assigned an infographic and ended up shipping the product. The part I would point to in an interview is not the code, it is killing our own subscription model once we were honest about how rarely anyone books a speaker. Knowing how a thing makes money is rarer than knowing how to build it.
Relevance. Pricing structure and channel design are my day job. Choosing commission over subscription, and routing payment through the platform so neither side has a reason to go around it, is the same call as pricing a distribution channel so partners stay in it. Podium Pal applied that thinking to an AI product instead of a sales route.

Evidence

The tool is live and open to anyone: abhyuday2103.github.io/mantrikai
