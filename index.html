About

Slab yard to data pipeline

I am a channel manager at Surfaces by Pacific, a wholesale stone and quartz distributor outside Chicago. I took a Wisconsin territory that was doing close to nothing and built it past one million dollars in annual revenue, mostly by showing up at fabrication shops until skeptical owners stopped being skeptical. Along the way I started building my own tools: a pricing calculator my sales team uses daily, a commission tracker, small scripts that answer questions faster than a spreadsheet can.

That habit is why I am here. I enrolled in the MS in AI and Data Analytics program at Indiana Wesleyan University to put real methods behind the instincts, because the long game is leading the international expansion of my family's consumer electronics manufacturing business in India, and I want to walk in with more than charm and a quota history. This portfolio is the working record of that transition. Nothing in it is polished for its own sake. Every artifact is something I actually made, with an honest note on what I got right and what I would do differently.

Workshop One

Artifact 01

Professional self-assessment

Why this is here. Before you can sell anyone else on a change, you have to run an honest audit on yourself. In Workshop One of AIML-500 I completed two diagnostic assessments, one on AI/ML integration readiness and one on change management, and then wrote about what the scores actually said. I am putting the full reflection here, unedited, because a portfolio that only shows wins is a brochure. This one starts with the gaps.

Completing these two assessments side by side was more revealing than I expected, mostly because of how differently I scored on each one.

On the AI/ML Integration assessment, my top three strengths were promoting the value of AI/ML within the organization, crafting a vision and strategy for adoption, and keeping up to date with advancements in the field. That pattern makes sense given my background. I spent years selling natural stone and quartz to fabricators in Wisconsin, and the job was essentially evangelism: convincing skeptical shop owners that a change was worth their time. Enrolling in this program while working full time is itself a bet on staying current. My top three growth areas were awareness of AI/ML ethical considerations, preparing employees for AI/ML-related changes, and understanding data requirements for AI/ML projects. The first one stung a little, but it is honest. I have built small tools at work, including a pricing calculator our sales team uses, and I never once asked what could go wrong with them ethically, only whether they worked.

On the Change Management assessment, my top three strengths were stakeholder engagement, performance evaluation, and problem-solving. Managing two direct reports has forced me to get good at reading where people actually are versus where they say they are, and building trust before asking for anything. My top three growth areas were knowledge of formal change management models and frameworks, risk management, and addressing resistance to change head-on. I have led change by instinct, but I could not have named a single framework before this course, and my instinct when someone resists is to route around them rather than through them. The Center for Creative Leadership (2024) calls this out directly, noting that unsuccessful change leaders avoid difficult stakeholders instead of influencing them, and I recognized myself in that line.

My opinion of the assessments as tools is mixed. Their real value is the one Change Strategists (2023) points to: diagnostic tools create a benchmark you can return to later, and identifying strengths matters as much as identifying gaps. But both forms share a structural weakness. They measure self-perception, not behavior. I could rate myself a 5 on everything and no one would know the difference. I also caught my own inconsistency, where I rated my awareness of ethical considerations a 1 but then scored 16 out of 20 on the dedicated ethics section, which tells me the overlapping items are not measuring cleanly. The Change Management form also runs long, with categories like problem-solving, critical thinking, and decision-making that blur into one another.

If I could revise them, I would make two changes grounded in the readings. First, every rating should require a one-sentence example, because Change Strategists (2023) argues that real assessment depends on behavioral evidence and past performance, and a bare number invites wishful thinking. Second, the AI/ML form should borrow the most important finding from the Center for Creative Leadership (2024) study and ask directly whether you communicate the "why" behind a change, not just the "what." The form asks whether I can explain AI/ML concepts and promote their value, but never whether I connect a change to what people already care about, and in my experience that is the entire game. I would also cut the redundant sections in the Change Management form and consolidate them under the nine competencies identified by the Center for Creative Leadership (2024), which are backed by research on 275 executives rather than a generic skills list.
